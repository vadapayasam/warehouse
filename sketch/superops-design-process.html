SuperOps Design Process

How the SuperOps design team thinks and works when designing any AI-native ITSM / MSP product surface. Goal-directed, reduced to essentials, obsessively persona-first, and AI-native by default.

Goal-Directed Design AI-Native ITSM Persona-First MSP + Client Layers Trust + Authority Theme

Scope Boundary

This skill is about how to think and what to decide. It deliberately does not cover visual styling, component choices, color, or tokens - those belong to the Super Kit design-system skill. When you reach the point of visual execution, hand off to that skill. Here, work in structure, behavior, states, and rationale.

Mindset - Hold These Before Touching a Layout

Design for the Goal, Not the Task (Goal-Directed Design)

People's goals - not their tasks - motivate their behavior. A technician's task is "close the ticket"; their goal is to stay unblocked, look competent to their MSP, and get through the queue without a mistake. Tasks are intermediate steps toward a goal; design for the end state the person actually wants. A design that satisfies business goals but ignores the person's goal will eventually fail.

Reduce

Start with clarity - state the problem and which personas are in the journey. Isolate the core issues; strip assumptions and unnecessary complexity. Pursue element minimalism: the core features that deliver value, nothing decorative.

Keep the User at the Center

Every element aligns to the persona and the objective. Aim for coherence across the surface, and treat the design as something you refine iteratively - never "final."

Model Two User Layers (Specific to MSP)

SuperOps serves the MSP (our customer) and the MSP's own clients (our customer's customer). Before designing, state which layer this surface is for and which persona within it - an MSP owner, an L1/L2 technician, and an end client have different goals and mental models and must not be blurred into one "user."

Process - Run This When Given a PRD, Brief, or Request

1. Discover - Where It Fits, Who It's For, Why

  • Locate the work in the product vision/strategy: what outcome is this really chasing, and how does it differentiate SuperOps?
  • Identify the persona(s) across both user layers, and for each, their most frequent and most important task, their day-in-the-life context, and the problems they face.
  • Answer the Goal-Directed questions: Who are the users? What are they trying to accomplish, and how do they think about it? What would feel rewarding? How should the product behave, take form, introduce itself, and handle problems - for both novice and expert?
  • State what "better" means as a measurable signal (e.g., fewer steps to resolve, faster time-to-first-action), so you design toward an outcome rather than a feeling.

2. Define - Frame the Right Problem

  • Identify the right problem beneath the PRD's proposed solution. The PRD often describes a feature; find the job underneath it.
  • Map the persona-based Jobs-To-Be-Done journey and anchor to the highest-value job.
  • Identify priorities: the critical features and the critical elements within each feature. Make every choice trace back to the persona + objective - this alone removes most complexity.
  • Write one benefit-first problem statement you can design against.

3. Design - Structure, Hierarchy, Completeness, Experience

Work in four passes. (Visual/component decisions defer to the Super Kit skill.)

Structure

Wireframe the idea, decide the layout, define the information architecture. Chunk content so it fits working memory (see UX laws).

Hierarchy & Interaction

Establish typography hierarchy, distinguish primary vs. secondary actions, use consistent UI elements and intuitive iconography, and create clear visual distinctions and grouping. Apply the UX laws below as reasoning tools here.

Completeness - Design Every State, Not Just the Happy Path

A screen is not designed until you've handled: data validation, state maintenance, loading states, empty states, error handling with a recovery path, and graceful degradation (the surface stays usable when something fails). Missing states are the most common failure - check them explicitly.

Experience Layers - What Makes It Feel World-Class

  • Efficiency & speed - streamline the persona's job to minimise steps and friction; prioritise fast access to primary information; automate repetitive tasks; give real-time feedback on every action.
  • Emotional engagement - responsive feedback that gives a sense of accomplishment, empathetic and user-centric copy, delightful details.
  • Trust & security - consistency/reliability of patterns; visible data protection; extra care and confirmation around destructive actions (e.g. deletion).
  • Contextual relevance & personalisation - adapt content and recommendations to the user's profile, behaviour, and vertical (e.g. medical, education, legal MSP clients).
  • Education & guidance - persona-based onboarding, in-app assistance (tooltips, tutorials, new-feature moments).
  • Differentiation - surface the USP; use visual storytelling to convey it.
  • Reach - responsive layout, accessibility, and plan-based feature gating where the product model requires it.

4. AI-Native Interaction - A Required Pass, Not an Afterthought

SuperOps is AI-native ITSM. For every AI touchpoint on the surface, decide and design:

  • Autonomy level on the gradient: suggest → draft → act-with-approval → act autonomously. Choose deliberately per task; don't default to full autonomy.
  • Explainability - show why the AI acted or recommended, traceable to its source, in a way the user can inspect and reverse. Treat this as layout, not a tooltip.
  • Human-in-the-loop & audit - for consequential actions, design the approval step and an audit trail.
  • Uncertainty & failure states - design for low confidence, missing data, and wrong answers, and always give the human a clean override.

5. Validate & Measure

  • Pressure-test the design against the persona's goal (not just its tasks) and against the quality gate below.
  • Note what should be validated with real users before build, and confirm the measurable signal from Discover can actually be observed once shipped.

UX Laws - Reasoning Heuristics

Use these to justify and pressure-test layout and interaction decisions. They're grouped by what they help you reason about.

Perception & Grouping (Gestalt)

LawApplication
Law of PragnanzThe eye prefers the simplest interpretation; favor simple shapes.
Law of ProximityPlace related things near each other; they read as a group.
Law of SimilarityStyle related elements alike; differentiate interactive elements consistently.
Law of Uniform ConnectednessConnect related functions with a shared color, line, or frame.
Law of Common RegionElements inside a shared boundary read as one group; use cards/containers to bind related data and controls (a ticket card, an asset panel).

Attention & Memory

LawApplication
Miller's LawWorking memory holds ~7+/-2; chunk content into groups of 5-9.
Serial Position EffectPeople recall the first and last items best; put the most important nav items and list entries at the ends, not buried in the middle.
Von Restorff (Isolation) EffectThe item that differs is the one remembered; make the single primary action and the USP visually distinct from everything around them.
Zeigarnik EffectOpen, unfinished tasks stay top of mind; use progress indicators and keep open tickets / incomplete onboarding visibly unresolved to pull the user back.

Action & Effort

LawApplication
Fitts's LawMake primary actions prominent and give them large, easy targets.
Hick's LawMore options slow decisions; break complex tasks into steps (a 4-stage / 5-option flow beats one 20-field form).
Jakob's LawUsers expect familiar patterns; reuse conventions so they don't relearn.
Tesler's Law (Conservation of Complexity)Every workflow has irreducible complexity, so decide who absorbs it. In AI-native ITSM, push it onto the system/agent - auto-triage, pre-filled fields, drafted replies - so the technician doesn't carry it.
Postel's Law (Robustness)Be liberal in what you accept, strict in what you emit; tolerate messy input (varied formats, natural-language queries to the AI) and return clean, predictable output.

Speed, Motivation & Feel

LawApplication
Doherty ThresholdKeep perceived response under ~400ms; when the AI needs longer, acknowledge instantly and stream or show a skeleton so momentum holds.
Goal-Gradient EffectMotivation rises as a goal nears; show progress (setup checklists, resolution steps) and shrink the perceived remaining effort toward the end.
Peak-End RuleAn experience is judged by its most intense moment and its ending; invest in the resolution moment and the close of a flow (ticket resolved, incident cleared), not just the average screen.
Aesthetic-Usability EffectPolished, coherent design is perceived as more usable and more trustworthy; craft drives adoption and buys goodwill that carries users past minor friction.
Parkinson's LawWork expands to fill the time given; constrain scope and surface only what matters so effort concentrates on the essential.
Pareto Principle (80/20)A small share of tasks drives most of the value; make the vital few paths effortless before polishing edge cases.

Foundations - The Canon This Process Thinks From

Four bodies of work underpin everything above. Reach for their concepts when a decision is hard.

Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD)

People "hire" a product to make progress in a specific circumstance - design for the job, not the feature or the demographic.

  • Frame the job as: When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome].
  • A job has functional, emotional, and social dimensions - a technician wants to clear the queue (functional), not look incompetent (emotional), and be seen as reliable by the MSP (social). Serve all three.
  • The real competition is anything that gets the job done - the old way, a spreadsheet, or doing nothing. Your AI competes with the technician's own muscle memory.
  • Forces of progress: increase the push of the current pain and the pull of the new way; reduce the anxiety of switching and the habit of the status quo. Onboarding and AI-trust design mostly fight anxiety and habit.

Don't Make Me Think (Krug)

Every question the interface raises - "where am I? what does this do? is this clickable?" - is cognitive tax. Remove it.

  • Make things self-evident; at worst, self-explanatory.
  • People scan rather than read, and they satisfice - they take the first workable option, not the best one. Make the obvious path the right one.
  • Cut copy hard: write it, remove half the words, then remove half of what's left. This matters most on dense ITSM screens.

The Design of Everyday Things (Norman)

Design so the system is understandable and forgiving.

  • Affordances & signifiers - make possible actions discoverable and clearly signal where to act.
  • Feedback - every action gets immediate, clear feedback (echoes the real-time-feedback pillar).
  • Mapping - controls should map naturally to their effects.
  • Constraints & forcing functions - limit actions to prevent error; guard destructive actions.
  • Conceptual model - give users an accurate model of how the system and the AI work, so behavior is never surprising.
  • The two gulfs - bridge the Gulf of Execution (can I tell how to do this?) and the Gulf of Evaluation (can I tell what happened and what state it's in?). For AI, the evaluation gulf is explainability.
  • Treat most "user error" as design error, and make actions reversible.

About Face / Goal-Directed Design (Cooper)

The source of this skill's mindset - design for goals, expressed through precise personas.

  • Commit to a persona; reject the "elastic user." "The user" as a vague everyone lets you justify anything - design for a specific primary persona.
  • Design for the perpetual intermediate - most people are neither novices nor experts for long; ease entry, enable mastery, but optimize the everyday middle.
  • Match the posture - a technician's console is sovereign (full attention, expert daily use → dense, keyboard-driven, efficient); an occasional client portal is transient (must be obvious and self-explaining); background agents are daemonic. Design to how the surface occupies attention.
  • Minimize excise - cut work that serves the tool rather than the goal: redundant steps, needless navigation, ceremony.
  • Be considerate - software should behave like a considerate, intelligent colleague, and forgive mistakes with generous undo.

What to Produce in a Design Session

  • A one-line, benefit-first problem statement + the persona and the layer (MSP vs. client) it serves.
  • The primary job and journey.
  • Flows / wireframes covering the happy path and the required states (loading, empty, error/recovery, validation, degraded).
  • Explicit AI-interaction decisions (autonomy, explainability, approval/audit, uncertainty).
  • Rationale that ties each significant decision back to the persona's goal and, where useful, a UX law.
  • A note on what to validate and the signal that proves it worked.

Then hand visual execution - components, tokens, styling - to the Super Kit design-system skill.

Never

  • Never design only the happy path. Missing states are the default failure.
  • Never blur the two user layers or "average" personas into one.
  • Never bolt on AI without deciding its autonomy level and how it explains itself.
  • Never make the user feel stupid, cause them to make big mistakes, demand too much effort, or ship something joyless - these are the four ways a design fails its user's goal.
  • Never optimise for the business goal at the expense of the person's goal; the business goal is best served by meeting the person's.
